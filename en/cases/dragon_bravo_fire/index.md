# Wildfire Damage Assessment for the Dragon Bravo Fire Using Satellite-Derived Burn Severity

The Dragon Bravo Fire was triggered by a lightning strike on July 4, 2025, on the North Rim of Grand Canyon National Park, Arizona. Following the ignition, fire management resources were quickly deployed to construct containment lines, initially holding the fire to a smaller footprint.

However, during the night of July 11, uncommon strong wind gusts from the northwest pushed the fire across existing containment lines, causing a rapid expansion of the fire area. Due to nighttime visibility limitations, aerial drops of retardant and water were suspended; however, ground crews and aircraft resumed high-intensity suppression operations during daylight hours, focusing on areas with the highest probability of success. The fire was managed under a full suppression strategy.

Persistent high temperatures, low humidity, and strong winds kept fire behavior highly active, impacting some infrastructure in the North Rim developed area. As the fire became progressively contained, resources transitioned into a suppression repair phase, stabilizing disturbed surfaces, cleaning up dozer lines, and restoring vegetation. A key focus was also clearing danger trees along Highway 67 and main roads leading to the North Rim to ensure safe public access.

This animation is based on Sentinel-2 multispectral satellite imagery, utilizing the differenced Normalized Burn Ratio (dNBR) calculated between pre- and post-fire observations to identify and extract the burned extent. dNBR is highly sensitive to vegetation damage and effectively maps the spatial distribution and evolution of the burn scar.

Following sustained suppression efforts, the Dragon Bravo Fire was declared 100% contained on September 28, 2025, and the North Rim of Grand Canyon National Park was partially reopened to the public on October 1. The fire ultimately consumed approximately 105,415 acres of vegetation.
