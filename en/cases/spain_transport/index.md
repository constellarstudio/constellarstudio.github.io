# Monitoring Transport Infrastructure Subsidence with Time-Series InSAR: A Case Study in the Valencia Region, Southeastern Spain

We observed widespread subsidence signals along highways and railways in the Villena urban area, with maximum subsidence rates reaching approximately 12 mm/yr. The high density of coherent pixels within this region and their contiguous spatial distribution indicate that this deformation represents a classic regional-scale subsidence process. The subsidence bowl centered on the highway and town areas demonstrates that InSAR effectively captures regional-scale ground deformation and reflects its comprehensive impact on transport infrastructure.

Simultaneously, several localized subsidence phenomena were identified. For instance, near the railway bridge south of La Font de la Figuera, a clear pattern of differential deformation is observed where the road embankments on both sides of the highway crossing over the railway are sinking, while the bridge main body remains stable. This difference indicates that subsidence is primarily occurring within the embankment structures, likely related to post-construction ground consolidation, whereas the bridge, utilizing deeper foundations, shows no significant movement. 

In contrast, another localized subsidence detected along the railway between La Encina and La Bodequeta exhibits an isolated spatial distribution with lower surrounding coherent pixel density, leading to some interpretation uncertainty. Furthermore, several discrete, point-like subsidence signals were detected along the highways, which may reflect localized foundation changes or early structural damage.

Overall, these results demonstrate that Sentinel-1-based InSAR time-series analysis can simultaneously detect regional-scale and localized ground deformation signals. In areas with high coherence (such as the Valencia region), this methodology is capable of resolving the differential response of different components of infrastructure (e.g., embankments vs. bridges). Therefore, InSAR technology holds significant potential for monitoring urban and transport infrastructure, providing early warning information for subsidence and damage during long-term operations.
