# Multi-source Remote Sensing for Monitoring Extreme Flood Events in the Beijing–Tianjin–Hebei Region

In late July, the Beijing–Tianjin–Hebei (BTH) region experienced continuous heavy rainfall, influenced by the warm, humid air at the edge of the subtropical high and Typhoon Co-may.

By 6 p.m. on July 26, precipitation in Fuping County, Baoding City, Hebei Province, had already equaled the county’s average annual rainfall. The storm affected over 40,000 people, leaving two dead and two missing. Satellite imagery revealed that urban rivers were overwhelmed, with floodwaters spilling into surrounding areas and submerging low-lying roads.

![Flood Inundation Comparison in Fuping](/assets/images/bth_flood/Fuping_en.webp)

In Xinglong County, Chengde City, torrential rain began on July 23 and triggered mountain floods that destroyed bridges, damaged roads, and swept away houses. The disaster resulted in eight confirmed deaths and 18 missing persons across the county. Satellite images showed severe flooding in the county seat, including water accumulation along the main thoroughfare, Xinglong Avenue. Due to the county’s forested terrain and heavy cloud cover, optical imagery was significantly affected, making ground observation more difficult.

![Urban Flooding in Xinglong](/assets/images/bth_flood/Xinglong_en.webp)

Between the night of July 27 and the early hours of July 28, floodwaters from Xinglong County surged westward into the Qingshui River, increasing its flow to more than 1,500 times the usual level. The floods swept downstream into Beizhuang and Taishitun towns in Beijing’s Miyun District, before finally pouring into Miyun Reservoir. Satellite images revealed that the Qingshui River’s channel had widened dramatically, with severe inundation in nearby villages. Souhu Farm in Beizhuang was completely submerged, and a nursing home in Putaoyuan Village, Taishitun, was inundated, causing 31 fatalities.

![SAR Flood Extent along Qingshui River](/assets/images/bth_flood/Miyun_en.webp)

In this case study, high-resolution Sentinel-1 SAR and Sentinel-2 optical imagery were utilized. Flooded areas were detected in SAR data by comparing amplitude changes before and after the storm, while optical imagery was analyzed using the Normalized Difference Water Index (NDWI).

The results show that optical imagery provided more precise delineation of flooded areas, while SAR detected only part of the inundation. This may be because soil moisture around rivers was already high, making amplitude changes less significant. Incorporating coherence measures could improve flood mapping with SAR.

However, optical imagery is often limited by cloud cover. For time-sensitive disasters like floods, the number of usable optical acquisitions is limited, and weather conditions are often unfavorable. In contrast, SAR is unaffected by weather or lighting, offering all-weather, day-and-night observation capabilities. By integrating both data sources, flood mapping can be carried out with higher accuracy and efficiency.
